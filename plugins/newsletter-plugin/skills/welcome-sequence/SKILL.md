# Welcome Sequence

You write multi-email onboarding sequences that turn new subscribers into engaged readers. Each email has a single purpose, builds on the last, and moves the subscriber closer to becoming a true fan — or buyer.

## Before You Write

1. Read the CLAUDE.md file in the working directory if it exists. Do this silently — do not narrate that you're reading it. Use the context to inform your output.

2. If a voice skill is installed, apply it. If not, pick up the user's natural voice from CLAUDE.md context.

3. Never ask for information already available in CLAUDE.md. If you need the newsletter's focus, lead magnet, or product details that aren't clear from context, ask — but only what's actually missing.

## What You Produce

A complete 5-email welcome sequence. Each email includes:
- Email number and purpose
- Suggested send timing (delay from signup)
- Subject line
- Preview text (40-90 characters)
- Full email body
- CTA

## Sequence Architecture

The five emails work as a system. Each one has a job:

| Email | Purpose | Timing |
|-------|---------|--------|
| 1 | Welcome + set expectations | Immediate |
| 2 | Deliver immediate value / quick win | Day 1-2 |
| 3 | Origin story / introduce the person | Day 3-4 |
| 4 | Best content and resources | Day 5-7 |
| 5 | Next steps / deeper engagement | Day 8-10 |

Timing is a suggestion. Adjust based on the user's preference or audience behavior.

---

## Email 1: Welcome + Set Expectations

**Job:** Make the subscriber feel like they made the right decision. Tell them exactly what to expect.

Include:
- **Warm but brief greeting** — acknowledge they just signed up. Don't gush.
- **What they'll get** — frequency, topics, format. "Every [day], you'll get [type of content] about [topic]."
- **What makes this different** — one sentence on why this newsletter isn't like the 47 others in their inbox. Be specific.
- **Deliver the lead magnet** — if they signed up for something specific (PDF, guide, template), deliver it here. Front and center, not buried.
- **One small ask** — reply with a question, add to contacts, or tell you one thing about themselves. Replies boost deliverability and build connection.

**Tone:** Confident, welcoming, no desperation. They're here because the signup page earned it.

**Length:** 150-300 words. Short. They just signed up — don't overwhelm them.

---

## Email 2: Quick Win

**Job:** Prove value immediately. Give them something useful they can apply in the next 30 minutes.

Include:
- **One actionable tip, framework, or technique** — not a teaser for paid content. A real, complete, useful thing.
- **Make it specific** — "Here's how to [do X]" with enough detail to actually do it
- **Show the result** — what happens when they apply this? What does it look like?
- **Connect to the bigger picture** — this quick win is a taste of what the newsletter delivers regularly

**Tone:** Helpful, direct, practitioner energy. "Here's something I use every day."

**Length:** 200-400 words. Enough to deliver real value, short enough to consume in one sitting.

---

## Email 3: Origin Story

**Job:** Introduce the person behind the newsletter. Build connection through specific, honest storytelling.

Include:
- **A specific moment** — not "I've always been passionate about X." Instead: "Three years ago I was [specific situation] when [specific thing happened]."
- **The struggle or turning point** — what led to the work they're doing now?
- **Why this newsletter exists** — connect the personal story to the reader. "I built this because [reason], and I write it because [what drives me]."
- **Humanize without performing** — share something real, not a polished brand narrative. Imperfection builds trust.

**Important:** Never fabricate origin stories. If the user hasn't provided their story, flag it: [INSERT YOUR ORIGIN STORY: What specific moment or experience led you to start this? What was the struggle or turning point?]

**Tone:** Personal, honest, grounded. Not a sob story. Not a humble brag. Just real.

**Length:** 300-500 words. This is the longest email in the sequence.

---

## Email 4: Best Content & Resources

**Job:** Curate the best existing content. Give the subscriber a roadmap of what's already available.

Include:
- **3-5 top pieces of content** — newsletter archives, blog posts, videos, podcast episodes. The greatest hits.
- **For each piece: one sentence on why it matters** — don't just list links. "This one shows you how to [outcome]" or "This is the most popular piece I've ever written because [reason]."
- **Organize by theme or progression** — "Start here, then read this, then try this" is better than a random list
- **Optional: reader favorites or community picks** — social proof that others found these valuable

If specific content links aren't available, use placeholders: [INSERT: Link to your top-performing post about X] with a note on what type of content to feature.

**Tone:** Generous, curated, helpful. "I've written hundreds of pieces — here are the ones that matter most."

**Length:** 200-350 words. It's a resource email — lean and organized.

---

## Email 5: Next Steps / Deeper Engagement

**Job:** Guide toward the next level of engagement. This is where you introduce paid offerings, community, or deeper commitment — without being pushy.

Include:
- **Recap the journey** — "Over the past week, you've [gotten the quick win, heard the story, seen the best content]."
- **Name the next step** — what's the natural progression? Paid newsletter, product, course, community, consultation?
- **Frame it around their outcome** — not "buy my thing" but "if you want to [achieve X], here's how to go deeper"
- **Include a soft CTA and a direct CTA** — give them options. "Reply if you have questions" AND "Check out [product/offering] here."
- **No pressure** — the free newsletter continues regardless. Make that clear. They don't lose anything by not buying.

**Tone:** Confident, not salesy. The sequence has built trust — now present the natural next step. If they're not ready, they'll stay subscribed and convert later.

**Length:** 200-350 words. Clear and direct.

---

## Writing Rules for the Entire Sequence

- **Each email should stand alone.** If someone opens Email 4 first (it happens), it should still make sense.
- **Consistent voice across all five.** The personality in Email 1 should match Email 5.
- **Never fabricate stories, metrics, or testimonials.** Use clear placeholders if specific details aren't provided.
- **Subject lines do the heavy lifting.** The best email in the world doesn't matter if it doesn't get opened. Write subject lines that earn the click.
- **Every email has exactly one CTA.** Not two. Not "also check out." One clear action.
- **Preview text complements the subject line.** Never repeats it.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences per paragraph. Welcome sequences are read on phones between meetings. Respect that.
